Pick Your Tribe

A map of the actual ideological tribes in British politics. The two-party structure conceals at least twelve distinct worldviews. Tap the ones you identify with — most people are surprised where they land.

Tap any card to claim it. You might find you belong to more than one — or to tribes on both "sides."

The Left

Seven worldviews, not all of them in the same party — or any party

Fabian Technocrats
Starmer, Reeves, Streeting, McFadden. Historically Blair, Brown, Mandelson. The Fabian Society, IPPR, Resolution Foundation.
Believe in managed progress through rational institutions. Meritocratic, data-driven, instinctively centrist. Their ideal society is basically Denmark. Comfortable with capitalism if properly regulated. Intellectually descended from the Webbs. Some are influenced by Effective Altruism's faith that suffering is calculable and progress measurable (Peter Singer, Will MacAskill) — though this is more a philosophical undercurrent than a parliamentary faction.
Moral framework: What produces the best outcomes for the most people.
The Identitarian Left
Kehinde Andrews, Priyamvada Gopal, Afua Hirsch, Reni Eddo-Lodge. Institutionally embedded in universities, HR departments, DEI consultancies, parts of the charity sector and the arts.
Intellectual roots not in the labour movement but in post-structuralism, critical race theory, and Foucault's analysis of power. The key move: liberal universalism and individual rights are themselves expressions of white, male, colonial power. Moral authority derives from positionality — where you sit in a matrix of oppression and privilege. Often conflated with the progressive left but the analysis is fundamentally different. A traditional socialist says the real divide is class; this tribe says it's race, gender, sexuality, and colonial history — and that class analysis can itself be a way of centring white male experience.
Moral framework: Your relationship to structures of oppression determines the authority of your voice.
Revolutionary Marxists / Anti-Imperialists
The SWP, Counterfire, George Galloway, John Rees, Lindsey German. The Morning Star. Stop the War Coalition. The Gaza independents in Parliament.
Materialist and structural — imperialism is the highest stage of capitalism, Western foreign policy is the root of global conflict, liberal democracy is a bourgeois facade. Share with the identitarian left a suspicion of liberal universalism, but for different reasons: not because universalism masks whiteness but because it masks capital. Many are religiously informed, drawing on Islamic or Christian traditions of justice. Crucially, this tribe regards revolutionary violence as potentially legitimate — which puts them at odds with every other tendency in the Labour coalition.
Moral framework: Are you on the side of the workers or of capital? Everything else is superstructure.
The Progressive Left (Corbynite)
Corbyn, McDonnell, Zarah Sultana, Nadia Whittome, Owen Jones. Novara Media, Tribune magazine.
Sits uncomfortably between several tribes. Economically democratic socialist — public ownership, redistribution, industrial strategy. On foreign policy, lean anti-imperialist. On social questions, aligned with the identitarian left. The fatal tension within Corbynism: its economic base in working-class communities was culturally conservative in ways its activist base in London and university towns was not. This is essentially what destroyed it electorally in 2019.
Moral framework: Solidarity — but stretched across economic radicalism and social progressivism in ways that don't always cohere.
Green-Adjacent Degrowthers
Caroline Lucas, George Monbiot, Jason Hickel. Extinction Rebellion, Just Stop Oil. The Green Party under Adrian Ramsay and Carla Denyer.
Material progress is the problem, but social progress — expanding autonomy, dismantling traditional hierarchies — is still the project. Radical austerity about consumption paired with radical liberalism about identity and social relations. Intellectual roots in deep ecology and post-material politics rather than the labour movement. The irony: telling people who've never had material security that the real project is consuming less is an easier sell in a seminar than in a working-class town.
Moral framework: The planet sets limits that override human desire. Growth itself is the enemy.
Blue Labour / Post-Liberals
Maurice Glasman, Jon Cruddas, Dan Carden, Paul Embery, Jonathan Rutherford, Shabana Mahmood. Intellectually rooted in Tawney, G.D.H. Cole, MacIntyre, Karl Polanyi.
Economically left, culturally conservative. Rooted in Christian socialism and trade union communitarianism. Suspicious of both free markets and progressive social liberalism. Material progress — lifting people out of poverty — is a genuine moral obligation. But the endless expansion of autonomous individual choice eventually undermines the communities that make good lives possible. Currently in flux: Glasman has moved toward alignment with MAGA and Reform, calling for Labour to build "on the left side of MAGA square." Whether this is the logical destination of Blue Labour or a betrayal of it is the live argument.
Moral framework: Human dignity is given by God — every person has equal worth, and that worth imposes obligations on society.
Union Pragmatists
Sharon Graham (Unite), Christina McAnea (Unison), Gary Smith (GMB). Historically Ernest Bevin.
Bread-and-butter materialism. Wages, conditions, pensions, job security. Often socially moderate or conservative in personal outlook. Instinctively suspicious of culture war politics on either side because it distracts from the things that actually matter to working people. Historically the backbone of Labour but increasingly alienated by both the managerialists and the activists.
Moral framework: Did your pay go up? Are your conditions safe? Everything else is noise.
The Right

Five worldviews and a mood

Burkean Traditionalists
The late Roger Scruton. Jesse Norman. Rory Stewart (now politically homeless). John Gray (writes for the New Statesman but belongs here intellectually).
Gradual reform, inherited institutions, the Crown, the Church, the countryside. Suspicious of ideology from any direction — including free market radicalism. Revere custom, continuity, and the wisdom of the dead. Nearly extinct as an organised parliamentary force — largely purged after Brexit.
Moral framework: We inherit obligations we didn't choose. Duty, custom, and continuity outweigh individual desire.
Thatcherite Free Marketeers
Liz Truss, Kwasi Kwarteng, Jacob Rees-Mogg, Kemi Badenoch (partly). The IEA, Adam Smith Institute, TaxPayers' Alliance. The Telegraph editorial line.
Low tax, deregulation, individual enterprise. Intellectually descended from Hayek and Friedman. Comfortable with globalisation, comfortable with creative destruction. Still powerful institutionally but the Truss premiership badly damaged the brand. Their fundamental weakness: no answer to the question "what holds a society together once the market has dissolved every traditional bond?"
Moral framework: Free exchange between consenting individuals is the foundation of a just society.
National Conservatives / Post-Liberal Right
Danny Kruger, Nick Timothy, Miriam Cates. Phillip Blond (ResPublica). UnHerd is their natural media home.
Sceptical of free markets, sceptical of mass immigration, interested in community, family, and national identity. Share a surprising amount of intellectual DNA with Blue Labour — both draw on MacIntyre and communitarianism — but arrive from a different starting point on redistribution and unions. Want an active state that supports families and domestic industry.
Moral framework: The nation is a real community with real obligations — not just an economic zone.
Reform-Adjacent Populists
Nigel Farage, Richard Tice, Lee Anderson, Ann Widdecombe. GB News is the media expression.
More a mood than a philosophy. Anti-establishment, anti-immigration, rhetorically anti-elite. Draw energy from genuine grievances about deindustrialisation and cultural dislocation but lack a coherent intellectual framework beyond opposition to the status quo. Currently the most electorally dynamic force on the right. The risk: the vacuum gets filled by whoever shouts loudest rather than whoever thinks most clearly.
Moral framework: The people vs the elite. A friend-enemy distinction that can be captured by left or right.
Libertarians / Free Speech Absolutists
Toby Young (Free Speech Union). Brendan O'Neill, Claire Fox (Spiked Online — former Revolutionary Communists turned libertarians, quite a journey). Some younger Spectator writers.
Small but vocal. Sceptical of the state in almost all domains. Overlap with Thatcherites on economics but push further. Philosophically closer to American conservatism than anything traditionally British. The Spiked crowd are an interesting case — they retain a Marxist analytical framework but have arrived at libertarian conclusions, which baffles everyone.
Moral framework: Leave people alone and let them choose. Individual sovereignty is sacred.

Case Study: The Assisted Dying Vote (Nov 2024)

When the tribes break free of party lines

The Terminally Ill Adults Bill was a free vote — no whip, pure conscience. The result shattered party lines and revealed the real tribal map. Danny Kruger (national conservative), Jeremy Corbyn (progressive left), Wes Streeting (Fabian), Kemi Badenoch (Thatcherite), and all the Gaza independents voted the same way — against. All Green MPs voted for.

Voted For (330)

  • ✦ Most Fabian technocrats
  • ✦ All Green MPs
  • ✦ Most progressive left
  • ✦ Many Thatcherites
  • ✦ Libertarians

Voted Against (275)

  • ✦ National conservatives (Kruger)
  • ✦ Blue Labour / post-liberals
  • ✦ All Gaza independents
  • ✦ Corbyn
  • ✦ Religious MPs across all parties
  • ✦ Disability rights advocates

The "against" coalition is the one hardest to explain on a left-right axis. What unites Kruger and Corbyn? A shared suspicion that individual autonomy isn't the highest political value — that offering death to vulnerable people in an unequal society is naive about how power operates. The poor and disabled will face subtle pressure that the wealthy never will. The compassionate response is better care, not an exit from a system that has failed people.

The "for" coalition is united by a different instinct: a competent adult's right to choose the manner of their death is a matter of personal liberty, and the state's role is to regulate that choice, not prohibit it.

Two completely different moral languages. One parliament.

Why Everyone Is Shouting Past Each Other

These tribes don't just disagree about policy. They disagree about what counts as a good reason for anything. A utilitarian thinks the right answer is whatever maximises wellbeing. An identitarian thinks it depends on who's speaking. A Christian socialist thinks human dignity is non-negotiable regardless of outcomes. A Burkean thinks what our ancestors built deserves deference. A libertarian thinks your freedom to choose trumps all of the above.

These frameworks are largely incommensurable — which is why political argument so often feels like people talking different languages. Alasdair MacIntyre diagnosed exactly this in After Virtue (1981). It's arguably got worse since.

The real fault lines don't run left to right. They run along different axes: communitarian vs individualist, globalist vs sovereigntist, and — as Christopher Lasch argued — those who believe in limits vs those who don't.

Notice who your unexpected neighbours are. Blue Labour and the national conservatives are closer to each other than either is to its own party leadership. Fabians and Cameroon Tories are essentially the same managerial class with different rosettes. The progressive left and the libertarians, who loathe each other, actually share a deep commitment to individual autonomy — they just disagree about which institutions are doing the oppressing.

Further Reading

Christopher Lasch — The True and Only Heaven (1991). The single best book on why "left vs right" no longer captures the real divide.

Alasdair MacIntyre — After Virtue (1981). Why modern moral argument is incoherent and what that means for politics.

Gary Dorrien — American Democratic Socialism (2021). The definitive history of secular and religious socialisms and where they diverge.

Paul Embery — Despised (2020). The Blue Labour case that the modern left loathes the working class.

Roger Scruton — How to Be a Conservative (2014). The Burkean case, elegantly made.

Eugene McCarraher — The Enchantments of Mammon (2019). Capitalism as religion — the Christian socialist critique at full stretch.
